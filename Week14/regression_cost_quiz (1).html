Question 1 of 10
What does MSE stand for, and how does it penalize errors?

✅ Correct Answer: B

Mean Squared Error (MSE) uses the formula: MSE = (1/n) × Σ(y_true - y_pred)²

Because errors are squared, large mistakes are penalized much more heavily. For example, an error of 10 contributes 100 to the loss, while an error of 2 only contributes 4. This makes MSE very sensitive to outliers.

Question 2 of 10
If your dataset has outliers due to measurement errors, which loss function would be most robust?

✅ Correct Answer: B

MAE (Mean Absolute Error) uses linear penalties: MAE = (1/n) × Σ|y_true - y_pred|

Unlike MSE which squares errors, MAE treats a 10-unit error as exactly 10× worse than a 1-unit error. This makes it robust to outliers because extreme values don't dominate the loss function. MAE predicts the median, which naturally ignores outliers.

Question 3 of 10
What does Huber Loss do that makes it a "hybrid" approach?

✅ Correct Answer: B

Huber Loss intelligently transitions between MSE and MAE based on a threshold δ (delta):

• When |error| ≤ δ: Uses 0.5 × error² (MSE) for precision

• When |error| > δ: Uses δ × |error| - 0.5×δ² (MAE) for robustness

This gives you the best of both worlds: accurate on normal data, robust to outliers!

Question 4 of 10
What does the R² (R-squared) score measure?

✅ Correct Answer: C

R² Score ranges from 0 to 1 (or negative if model is worse than baseline) and measures: R² = 1 - (SS_residual / SS_total)

Interpretation:

• R² = 0.85 means your model explains 85% of the variance in the data

• The remaining 15% is due to noise, missing features, or randomness

• R² = 1.0 would be a perfect model (rarely achieved in practice)

Question 5 of 10
In Quantile Regression with τ = 0.9 (90th percentile), what does the model predict?

✅ Correct Answer: B

Quantile Regression (τ=0.9) predicts the 90th percentile, meaning:

• 90% of actual values will be at or below this prediction

• Only 10% will exceed it

• This is an optimistic upper bound - useful for SLA guarantees

Example: "Your package will arrive in 5 days or less (90% confidence)"

Question 6 of 10
Your model has Train MSE = 10 and Test MSE = 120. What does this indicate?

✅ Correct Answer: C

12× gap between train and test performance is a huge red flag!

Healthy ratios:

• 0.9-1.1× = Excellent generalization ✅

• 1.1-1.5× = Good (slight overfitting) ✅

• 1.5-2.0× = Warning (needs attention) ⚠️

• 2.0-12.0× = Severe overfitting (like your case!) ❌

Fix: Remove outliers, add more data, use regularization, or cross-validation

Question 7 of 10
Which loss function predicts the MEDIAN instead of the MEAN?

✅ Correct Answer: B

MAE minimizes absolute deviations, which mathematically leads to predicting the median.

Why this matters:

• Data: [10, 20, 25, 30, 1000] (outlier!)

• Mean = 217 (skewed by outlier)

• Median = 25 (robust to outlier)

MAE and Quantile(τ=0.5) are equivalent and both predict the median, making them ideal for skewed distributions!

Question 8 of 10
What is the δ (delta) parameter in Huber Loss typically set to?

✅ Correct Answer: B

δ = 1.35 is the default because it achieves 95% statistical efficiency compared to MSE on Gaussian data.

How to choose δ:

• δ = 1.0: More robust (switches to MAE sooner) - use if 5-10% outliers

• δ = 1.35: Balanced (default) - optimal for most cases

• δ = 2.0: Less robust (stays in MSE longer) - use for clean data

Smaller δ = more like MAE, Larger δ = more like MSE

Question 9 of 10
You need to predict delivery times with an SLA guarantee ("95% arrive within X days"). Which approach is best?

✅ Correct Answer: C

Quantile Regression (τ=0.95) directly predicts the 95th percentile.

Why this works:

• Predicting "5 days" means 95% of deliveries arrive in ≤5 days

• Only 5% will be late (acceptable for SLA)

• This is mathematically optimal - you can't do better!

Wrong approaches: Multiplying MSE predictions by constants doesn't capture the true uncertainty distribution.

Question 10 of 10
When would you choose MSE over Huber Loss in production?

✅ Correct Answer: B

Choose MSE when:

✅ Data is guaranteed clean (no outliers)

✅ You want maximum precision (lowest possible error)

✅ Large errors are genuinely important to penalize heavily

✅ Example: Calibrated sensor data in a controlled environment

Choose Huber when:

✅ Data quality is uncertain (production systems)

✅ You need robustness against occasional bad data

✅ You want "insurance" without sacrificing much precision

Reality: Huber is usually the safer choice in production! Only use pure MSE if you're 100% confident in data quality.
